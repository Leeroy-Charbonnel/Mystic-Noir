Samuel's investigation first leads him to the police station where he meets with Detective Collins, an old colleague who provides him with grudging access to the case files. The files reveal the killer's pattern - women in their twenties, all with some musical background, though none were professional musicians. Each crime scene contained sophisticated recording equipment, and in three cases, vinyl records were sent to the police afterward, containing the victim's final moments mixed with strange musical accompaniment.

While visiting the <a data-href="Stardust Lounge" href="Stardust Lounge" class="internal-link" target="_blank" rel="noopener nofollow">Stardust Lounge</a> again to question lounge singer Eliza Crane, who knew one of the victims, Samuel encounters Victor Monrose, the club's owner. Monrose seems unusually interested in the case but provides an alibi for the nights of the murders. Eliza reveals that the victim had mentioned meeting a refined gentleman who was fascinated with her voice and wanted to 'capture its essence' for posterity.

Following leads about unusual purchases of recording equipment, Samuel discovers a pattern of deliveries to various abandoned properties throughout the city. At one location - an old theater in the French Quarter - he finds evidence of recent use: cigarette butts of an expensive foreign brand, musical notation sheets with disturbing annotations about 'pitch of fear' and 'resonance of terror', and a single black glove of fine quality.

Samuel consults Dr. Lawrence Blackwell, an expert in music theory and psychology at Tulane University, who explains the concept of 'musical obsession' - a rare psychological condition where individuals believe certain sound combinations hold mystical or transcendent power. As Samuel continues investigating, he notices he's being followed. After a tense chase through the French Quarter, he confronts his stalker - Officer Jenkins, who admits Detective Collins assigned him to track Samuel's movements. Collins suspects Samuel might be withholding information, creating tension between the police investigation and Samuel's private inquiry.

A breakthrough comes when Samuel realizes the locations of the victims' disappearances, when mapped, form a musical staff across New Orleans. By plotting the next logical point in the pattern, he identifies a potential target area for the killer's next victim. Setting up surveillance, Samuel witnesses a well-dressed man approaching young women in the predicted zone.

Tracking the suspect leads Samuel to an underground recording studio hidden beneath an abandoned jazz club. There he discovers walls covered with musical notation, anatomical drawings of human vocal cords, and a modified piano with horrifying components that appear to be human in origin. Most disturbing is a journal detailing the killer's descent into madness - his belief that the perfect combination of musical notes and human vocalization will open a gateway to reunite him with his deceased wife, a renowned opera singer.

When Samuel discovers Marianne Thompson is still alive but held captive for an elaborate recording 'session', he must race against time to save her before the killer completes his magnum opus.
